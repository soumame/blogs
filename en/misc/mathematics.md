I started [[en/works/keio|university]] in 2025 and the first semester has already finished, but in several classes I'm taking in the second semester, knowledge of high school math is required, and I'm in a pretty bad spot.

> Calculus and stuff show up at university and I'm paying the price for not having studied them in high school... (In the Australian curriculum they are elective subjects, and I only took the equivalent of Mathematics I and A) It's strange that I haven't needed them even when writing code until now.
> 
[Quote from X](https://x.com/So_to9/status/2068907852870418614)

I attended [[en/works/pisa|an Australian international school in Malaysia]], and as part of the Victoria curriculum (VCE) I took a subject called "General Mathematics." In this curriculum, mathematics has three types:
- General Mathematics 
- Mathematical Methods
- Specialist Mathematics 

You can tell from the names, but General Mathematics is the basic level, and Specialist is for people who like math and study it more seriously. I viewed math as a tool and didn't like it that much, so I took General Mathematics.

The scope covered by General Mathematics corresponds to Mathematics I and Mathematics A in Japan. The main features are that the use of a CAS calculator — a graphing calculator — is assumed, and that it includes financial mathematics, matrices, and network theory, which are not present in Japan.

For those aiming for science/engineering, taking Mathematical Methods is mandatory, and without it you may not be able to enter certain science faculties. This covers the equivalents of Mathematics II, B, and C.

Specialist Mathematics is the most advanced one, often taken by those aiming for engineering, and it seems to be at the level of Mathematics III plus university-level introductory math.

I love engineering and am technically a science student, but I took General, so I'm still struggling quite a bit...

But I don't think this system is bad; I actually think it might be better than Japan's. In Japan, even humanities students need to reach a fairly high level, and in general high school tracks they probably have to cover up to something like the Mathematical Methods level. This does contribute to raising the overall math level of the Japanese population, but if it's all crammed in and ends up forgotten, I feel the effect of doing it is reduced.

Also, the financial math covered in General Mathematics is quite useful — I recall it when doing ordinary things like investing in mutual funds or depositing money — so I'm glad I had it.

Also personally, I think it was pretty good that I studied network theory and matrices earlier; they often come in handy. In Japan, these are expressed using formulas, but in Australia, even though there aren't many formulas, you still encounter calculations in network theory and matrices. They even skip the calculation details and just input them into the calculator. In General Mathematics, math is treated as a tool for everyday life.
