## I participated in a talk session at the Minecraft Cup
I recently appeared in a talk session at the [7th Minecraft Cup](https://minecraftcup.com/) held at the University of Tokyo (Interfaculty Initiative in Information Studies, Fukutake Hall), where I discussed "What Minecraft teaches us and the power of creativity"—talking about what I gained through Minecraft.
### What is the Minecraft Cup
> [[en/misc/minecraftcup|Minecraft Cup]] is a digital making contest held in Japan for children from elementary to high school age, where participants use Minecraft: Education Edition to create works based on themes and compete by submitting entries from across Japan and overseas.

Alongside announcing that I appeared in the talk session, I wanted to leave a record here about my relationship with the Minecraft Cup and what I gained from Minecraft (especially from the Minecraft Cup).

At the time of this session, a rough way to describe my background related to the Minecraft Cup is: a software engineer who enrolled in [[en/works/keio|Keio University, Faculty of Environment and Information Studies (SFC)]] after winning awards at the Minecraft Cup. Even saying "software engineer" feels a bit outdated now that AI can write code, so such titles may fade.

I had been working part-time at the Minecraft Cup ([ [ja/works/dmcouncil|Digital Making Council] ]) for the past few months, so while I spoke as a guest, I was actually helping behind the scenes as well. ## My connection with the Minecraft Cup

[![Image from Gyazo](https://i.gyazo.com/5058cee0ce7389a8b62895f86addd105.jpg)](https://gyazo.com/5058cee0ce7389a8b62895f86addd105)
I won an award at the Minecraft Cup in [[en/accomplishments/minecraftcup-2021|2021]]. I've also had rounds that didn't end in awards ([[en/accomplishments/minecraftcup-2023|2023]]) and years when I made a project but couldn't submit it (2019). Sometimes things go well, sometimes they don't. The Minecraft Cup (especially the town development category) is primarily a team competition, and teams can break down due to disagreements, poor coordination, or loss of motivation—so the only time my team really succeeded was in 2021.

https://minecraftcup.com

### What kind of team was it?
We had a mix of people: those strong in technical skills, those good at design and planning, and those with broad but shallow knowledge beyond Minecraft. We were a team of people who liked "digital making," which is literally in the organizing group's name.

Also, the team was made up of people with different backgrounds: some didn't attend school, some couldn't do what most people consider "normal," and others had extraordinary talents. I myself tended to skip school back then.

Was that a bad thing? Not necessarily. In 2021, during COVID, with online classes and more free time, it created tailwinds that allowed the whole team to happily enjoy digital making in Minecraft.

Another characteristic was that we didn't have an instructor at the start. It began as friends playing a game together and gradually formed into a team. We didn't attend programming schools or get forced into it—just friends who liked the game got together, found this opportunity, jumped in, and met many kind adults who helped us grow. My parents weren't trying to push me into Minecraft, but they weren't totally opposed either. They said things like "games only 30 minutes a day"—a bit like Kagawa Prefecture's ordinance—but they were thinking about what was best. In the end, the environment satisfied my intellectual curiosity, and I was lucky for that.


## Effects of the Minecraft Cup
So what happened afterward? Amazingly, I'm now a university student. I'm attending [[en/works/keio|Keio University, Faculty of Environment and Information Studies (SFC)]], a place where someone who struggled at school can now attend almost perfectly—it's fun.

I wasn't great at typical school studies (the usual classes up to high school), but I knew I could study endlessly when it was something I loved. These kinds of personal traits are now more recognized and can be leveraged in university admissions (called AO admissions at Keio). University is truly wonderful: you can study and delve deeply into what you love and are interested in.

### What happened after the Minecraft Cup
Since the Minecraft Cup, my main path has been:
- Starting with a part-time job at a programming school ([[en/works/yago.inc|part-time work at a programming class]])
- Then applying for and being accepted into [[en/accomplishments/mitoujr-2024|Mitou Junior]], based on issues I learned about through programming education, and winning an award at [[en/accomplishments/teens-apps-awards|App Koshien 2024]] with a project I made there
- Afterwards, enrolling in [[en/works/keio|Keio University, Faculty of Environment and Information Studies (SFC)]]

I think it's a great outcome that what I gained from the Minecraft Cup has taken shape in these ways. Of course the Minecraft Cup isn't everything, and I've met many people outside of it, but it's definitely one of the contributing factors.
