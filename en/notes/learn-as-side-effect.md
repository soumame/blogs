## With the Arrival of AI, What Effects Will There Be?

Honestly, recently I've felt that these kinds of realizations have been happening a bit less.

soumame
> For instance, there's an app I'm building at [[en/works/keio|my university]], and I've had AI implement more than half of it. But sometimes I'm missing the deep understanding I should have, and when that's pointed out, I end up saying, "AI made it, so I don't know."

This is seriously a major problem to me. But we can't stop it.

Companies and researchers use it as a matter of course, and at least in fields like software development, the necessary costs and your personal time are overwhelmingly reduced. When those around you are using AI, you can't be the only one not using it and writing code by hand.

However, the joy of writing code and those sudden insights you get while thinking might have disappeared along with the coding experience itself.

soumame
> When I did [[en/accomplishments/mitoujr-2024|Mitou Junior]] in 2024, ChatGPT and such were still pretty dumb and couldn't autonomously write code—good old times...
> People were saying things like, "GPT-4 is too smart, this will change the world," "Google Bard is being renamed to Gemini, how long will it last?", or "Anthropic released Claude 3 Haiku"... it was a noisy time.

Back then there weren't many coding agents and they weren't very useful, which I miss. You had to watch the code AI wrote, copy-paste it, and think about why it worked. Because AI was unreliable, you made a lot of detours.

But when did it start? Before I knew it, I feel like I've been leaving everything to AI.

## What Does Learning Mean...?

It's becoming unclear whether studying technology will be useful in society. There are areas AI isn't great at yet (hardware development, low-level work, OS-related stuff) even within what I know, but I think it's only a matter of time before those areas are no longer safe.

### I Don't Even Know What I Want to Research

I once tried using LLMs for programming education and experimented a lot, thinking I might continue that at university. I even wrote that in my application. But what happened? A few months after starting university, AI could already make things similar to what I'd been building.

Of course making things is fun. But I feel the enjoyment of the creation process has significantly diminished, and because I let AI do it, the byproducts I used to get when I did it myself have disappeared.

So how should I acquire abilities? That's a very hard question. If you just let AI do everything, at worst you'll be unable to solve problems yourself and become entirely dependent on AI. Society might still run on that, though.

soumame
> Maybe even if everyone becomes neutered by AI, if everyone uses it society will still function similarly.

But is it okay to become dependent on AI?

soumame
> We can't do anything without computers, and if they suddenly disappeared, humans probably couldn't do anything. I'm wondering if the dependency will be at that same level.

I don't think dependency itself is inherently bad if it makes things better. What gets better? Human technical skill? Human happiness?

I've written about [[en/notes/why-human-move|why people move]], but at this rate I might lose track of why I'm alive. What do I live for?

## Should I Take the Troublesome Path?

In the end, lately I've been thinking I should take the tedious path to keep enjoying creating things. Try deliberately not using AI, or bombard AI with questions. That way you can enjoy the process, and the sense of accomplishment when you succeed is enormous.

### Because of Capitalism

soumame
> I admit I don't understand money systems well, so I'm not sure if this is correct.

In the end, I think capitalism plays a role in this dependency. If you sell what you make you get money, and money buys things. So you use AI to make things faster and cheaper in shorter time spans, sell them, make more money, and accelerate growth.

The same thing happens in schools. When homework is assigned, everyone throws it into AI as a matter of course. Teachers tell students not to use AI, so students use paraphrasing tools to avoid detection and present it as their own answer. But they learn nothing. Still, everyone keeps doing it because grades determine paths in life, and grades affect future opportunities.

soumame
> Basically, people think they'll get better scores if they use AI.

Even if you graduate with high scores without learning anything, at least in today's Japan that's accepted. Many jobs don't use the knowledge you learned at university, and companies don't hire solely for knowledge. In fact, because new hires lack extra ingrained information, companies can hire them as fresh graduates and tune them to their needs.

If there's such a convenient thing, money will concentrate there, and capitalism lets resources get concentrated in one place.

### Falling Behind by Detours

Honestly, I want to enjoy the tedium, but I feel like I can't take many detours. To be frank, I sometimes have AI do my university assignments.

soumame
> Even though I know I should spend time on them, I have things I want to do... wait, then what's the point of going to university?

Fortunately, the other things I want to do are related to research groups, so they're still within the university. [[en/works/keio|The university I'm at]] allows interdisciplinary work, and from the first year you can (if you have one) do what you like, so it might be relatively easy to find things you want to do later within the university.

That said, by skipping tedious classes I don't want to take but am taking for credits via AI, I might be missing things I could have discovered there.

In a short life that's forced into competition, should you take detours? But being human is what allows detours in the first place...

soumame
> Then again, it's just that current LLMs can't do it, and eventually something will come that thinks about lots of unrelated things and makes new discoveries...

But in a world where aimless detours are hard to get credit for and concrete results are demanded, it's tough. AI is already expert in certain fields, so maybe as a collective human intelligence we should wander and make discoveries by meandering. But without goals it's also difficult.

## So What Am I Trying to Say?

Maybe I just want to detour more and I find that enjoyable. But the world is highly competitive (as it always is), and recently AI has started to take over tasks humans used to do, so I think we can no longer find new things by taking those tedious detours we used to while thinking. So really, I want to live a life of taking such detours, but the chances of not producing concrete results are high. If one day AI produces results instead of humans, maybe we should think on the tedious processes AI doesn't want to do—while taking detours—and try to steer society to change that way. Also, if we change the system so people are rewarded for living well rather than only for producing outputs, maybe people could live happily. (There would be considerable side effects, so it's not necessarily good overall.)
