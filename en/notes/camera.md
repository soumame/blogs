I used to say [[en/misc/wishlist-2025-12|I can’t buy the things I want]], but I started a [[en/works/diver-x|part-time job]] and, while being allowed to do what I want, I’ve been slowly saving money.
So, I’m thinking of buying a camera.

What? Aren’t you going to buy VR? ~~I can’t say something like "I won’t buy it for now because there’s a BigScreen at my workplace and nothing else feels like VR anymore,"~~ but since I’ve sold VR gear before, I guess a camera comes first.

My budget is within 150,000 yen. By "camera" I don’t mean smartphone-level — I want something that gives a nice bokeh, satisfies a bit of ownership desire, and won’t prevent me from shooting things because of its performance.

## Cameras I’ve bought in the past
First, before buying a new one, let’s look back at the past.

### IXY 130
In 2014, when I was starting to get really interested in a lot of things, my parents bought me one as a birthday/Christmas present (my birthday is December 15, so presents often "merge"). It was 12,000 yen. A 12,000-yen camera for an eight-year-old boy is not cheap.

Simple operation, limited functions, but unlike a toy camera for kids it actually took decent photos and had an optical zoom.
It was a typical compact digital camera; the image quality is below today’s smartphone cameras, but it’s an important memory.
[![Image from Gyazo](https://i.gyazo.com/322cacd716bb48055bdf9c2b666f952f.jpg)](https://gyazo.com/322cacd716bb48055bdf9c2b666f952f)
The first photo I took that I could show people was from a trip to Hokkaido during third grade spring break. I remember the excitement of being on a plane. Back then my parents used JAL award tickets to take me, so flying was comfortable and thrilling. That’s a far cry from now when I ride six hours on AirAsia (a Southeast Asian international LCC) and slowly lose feeling in my butt.

I didn’t carry a smartphone around all the time back then, so this was basically the only way I intentionally recorded things in image form.


### Power shot SX720 HS
[![Image from Gyazo](https://i.gyazo.com/de901908de04fc7c8a591a6644d08809.jpg)](https://gyazo.com/de901908de04fc7c8a591a6644d08809)
After that, in 2019. After using the IXY for five years I had gotten pickier and started to feel its performance was lacking.
Again it was a Christmas/birthday combined present from my parents. 36,000 yen. I really admire my parents for buying a camera that expensive for a middle schooler (I didn’t understand how hard it was to earn money back then).

[![Image from Gyazo](https://i.gyazo.com/25c60a06c0dd6eb80c73d5bbd01ba5b4.jpg)](https://gyazo.com/25c60a06c0dd6eb80c73d5bbd01ba5b4)
Maybe I discovered train photography as the image quality improved. The E217 series feels nostalgic. That line doesn’t run anymore. Telephoto is great.

I remember once dropping this camera in water and having to get it repaired.

But I didn’t use it as much as the IXY130.
When I became a middle schooler my parents bought me a smartphone, which covered most things. My first smartphone was an iPhone 7 (the kind the carrier practically gave away), and I was amazed by its performance. Compared to a compact camera that cost over 30,000 yen — now that I think about it, that’s about entry-level — smartphone built-in cameras were becoming competitive.
It didn’t produce any bokeh, and to be frank, the parts where it won were...
But this camera did have a palm-sized body with 40x optical zoom, which was incredibly useful.

After that I started using phones like the POCO X3 and the Google Pixel 6, which had lots of cameras, and I stopped using dedicated cameras as much.


### Nikon D40
Using smartphones made me realize how much bokeh changes a photo’s impression, and I started wanting a way to achieve that. Smartphone AI corrections and portrait modes detect subjects and force blur, but it still feels very different from the optical bokeh that occurs physically.

From the closet came a dust-covered DSLR, a Nikon D40. It was something my parents used long ago.

[![Image from Gyazo](https://i.gyazo.com/ed1c75a14b9cdc2befc6441878f848b2.jpg)](https://gyazo.com/ed1c75a14b9cdc2befc6441878f848b2)
A photo from when I inexplicably disassembled a DVD drive.
The bokeh really is nice.

It was in pretty good condition and all functions worked perfectly. I used it somewhat to shoot small objects. Due to the sensor characteristics, the colors had a film-like, so-called "moody" look (lack of vocabulary). Still, AF is present but practically manual, and it’s weak in low light — the noise is intense. I didn’t take it out of the house too often. Tweaking settings, bracing to avoid camera shake, worrying about lighting, and then pressing the shutter can be fun in its own way.


### Osmo Pocket 3
I might have started to seriously think about "cameras" in 2024.
I had been satisfied with smartphone cameras, but when I was filming things I made and presentations for [[en/accomplishments/mitoujr-2024|Mitou Junior]] to share with the world (on the internet), I started to feel some limits to my phone camera. If you film for 30 minutes, the phone will stop recording.

So I bought an Osmo Pocket 3 with the Mitou Junior budget. At the time I was live-streaming announcement events, and this camera was helpful then. It’s still very useful and I carry it everywhere, so the body now has a crack in it 😱

[![Image from Gyazo](https://i.gyazo.com/36b6beb118f4e4e789925624533f4569.jpg)](https://gyazo.com/36b6beb118f4e4e789925624533f4569)

This camera is really easy to use and small, so it’s still often in my bag. The UX is just too good. You can’t do a lot of fine-grained settings, but you can quickly shoot video. For stills, in some cases it might even beat a smartphone camera. Of course it has no wide-angle or telephoto (there are wide-angle attachments), and it doesn’t have the insanely powerful AI corrections like a recent Pixel that can make nonexistent objects appear.


### Pixel 9 Pro
Maybe it was with the Pixel 9 that I felt we had entered an era where a smartphone alone could capture what I wanted.
It was the first phone I bought with my own money after graduating high school.

The Pixel 9 Pro is extremely good, and I really like its HDR feature. The preview on the phone even shows brightness information for the photos you take.

[![Image from Gyazo](https://i.gyazo.com/51c630f0ddbe500ff46fe017e51b960c.jpg)](https://gyazo.com/51c630f0ddbe500ff46fe017e51b960c)
This is from a recent trip to Odawara. If you use an HDR-capable device you should see parts of the image brighten.

To be honest, for close range this is fine and I’m pretty satisfied. The ability to quickly take a shot when you want to is great. There aren’t many settings to tweak and everything is automatic, but in an era when people take lots of photos and post them without editing, this can be preferable.

## I want to take even better photos
My current setup is good enough, but sometimes I want to take even better still photos. When people around me bring cameras and let me try them, the difference from a smartphone camera is clear.

I’ve been wavering about this for about a year, and I’ve set aside roughly 150,000 yen as money to buy a camera.

My parents often tell me I spend money recklessly or that I’m being extravagant, and my family isn’t the type to spend hundreds of thousands on hobbies. Maybe we don’t have many long-lasting hobbies or entertainments in my family. Or maybe I just haven’t seen it much. Anyway, because there weren’t people like that in my family, I hesitated to buy. There are other reasons too.

It’s not like you need these things to live, and even if they’re not profitable, I still think they can enrich your life.

So, I’m thinking of buying a camera. In short, material desire. And I think cameras have a strong "pay to win" element, so I want an interchangeable-lens camera.

### Choosing my first camera
I don’t know much about cameras, so I thought an entry-level model would be good.
However, I’d already had chances to use high-end gear like a friend’s SONY α7IV, the BlackMagic used at [[en/works/dmcouncil|events when we were short-staffed]], the ROS R5 (I think?), and the EOS R6 Mark II borrowed from [[en/works/keio|university]]. In other words, I had already touched higher-end equipment. So when I handled cheaper entry models in stores, I felt the gap strongly.

That made choosing for myself very difficult. I live at home, so I can save a lot of my part-time wages, but given the money I’ve saved, about 150,000 yen including a kit lens is about my limit.

When I looked, it came down to:
- Nikon Z50
- EOS R50
- Nikon Zfc
- Sony a6400
There are other options, but in terms of reputation, balance, and cost performance, these seemed about right.

Among them, the EOS R50 felt like the best value by far. I handled it in a store and its AF speed is close to higher-end models, and it’s also relatively new. Personally I prefer Nikon’s mechanical feel, but for the convenience of tossing it in the back of my bag and taking it anywhere, I ended up leaning toward the EOS R50. That means I bought in the order IXY, Power shot, EOS as brands. I was curious about other manufacturers, but I might settle on Canon after all.

## Going to buy the camera
At first I thought I’d just buy it on Amazon, but I’m still 19 and my card limit won’t go above 100,000 yen so I couldn’t pay with it, and I saw a sale at an electronics retailer, so I decided to buy there. Also, cheap Amazon listings are usually marketplace sellers, and their warranty situations looked sketchy, so I felt electronics retailers are strong for this kind of purchase.

I chose a kit with two lenses (a zoom lens and a telephoto).

The price at the electronics store was about 125,000 yen. That felt cheap for two lenses. With cashback it was even cheaper. I passed on the Amazon marketplace and went to an electronics store I hadn’t been to in a while.

It took a few hours to decide whether to buy points and peripheral camera items together.
