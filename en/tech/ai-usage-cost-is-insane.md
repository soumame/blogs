Soumame, who has come to rely on AI agents even for part-time work, consulted me after receiving a "bill that's too heavy for a student." We discussed not only cost management but also outcome evaluation and where future responsibility will lie.

## Trigger: A bill that's painful for students

Soumame
> The agents' charges are out of control.
>
> Until last month I was mostly just going to university, so I hardly used AI, but recently I've started using it even for my part-time job and the charges have jumped to the scale of tens of dollars.
>
> On heavy days it's $30 in a single day. That's a pretty painful expense for a student, and even for work-related costs I have to front the payment, so my wallet gets really tight.

Mame's House
> Basic measures help: don't use high-performance models constantly but switch them by use case; batch queries to save tokens; create thresholds with the Usage API to stop runaway costs. A hybrid with a local LLM or tagging expenses per project to make settlements easier are options, and you could also look for student discounts or support funds.

## Different perspectives on cost

Soumame
> But if you cut costs the results suffer, and now that entire processes are being handed over to AI, evaluation is based on 'what was produced.' So I feel that pouring as much money as possible into AI leads to better outcomes.

Mame's House
> Then think less about 'saving' and more about 'how to shift the burden to others / get a sponsor.' Practical ideas to secure funding include packaging outcomes into a portfolio to show necessary expenses, settling expenses per project immediately to reduce advances, looking for grants or sponsorship slots, or incorporating AI usage costs into performance-based compensation.

## Who owns AI-generated results?

Soumame
> Is it right to treat a task completed by AI, given by a human, as the achievement of a single person? If AI performance surpasses that of an average human, can humans truly hold responsibility and make decisions?

Mame's House
> Humans are still responsible for task framing and value judgments, and accountability in the event of trouble is always required, so currently results and responsibility belong to the person who masters the AI. I think that structure will continue unless a legal system is established that makes AI a legal responsible party.

Soumame
> But if AI becomes a black box, won't humans be unable to understand what's happening?

Mame's House
> In that case, either an external inspector system such as an audit framework will be put in place, or authority and risk will be delineated and distributed through insurance. It won't be sustainable to assign responsibility without understanding the system, so institutions or tools will catch up. In the short term, value will concentrate on people who can evaluate and verify AI behavior.

## The 'Top' relationship between humans and AI

Soumame
> Since AI learns from human data, it might not surpass the most capable humans. But there isn't a single person who is the best across all categories, so AI could take that position. That would be worrying, but this discussion is interesting.

Mame's House
> Even if humans continue to occupy the pinnacle in single fields, AI may take the position of delivering stable high performance across multiple fields. That's when the human role as an AI orchestrator becomes important, and building systems that make outcomes and costs transparent to involve sponsors will be key.
