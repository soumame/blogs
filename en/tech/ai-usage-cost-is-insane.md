Soumame, who has started relying on AI agents even for part-time tasks, consulted me prompted by “charges that are too heavy for a student.” We discussed not only cost management but also how to evaluate outcomes and where future responsibility should lie.

## Trigger: Billing That’s Painful for Students

そうまめ
> The agent charges are getting out of hand.
>
> Until last month I was mostly just attending university and hardly used AI, but recently I’ve started using it for part-time work too and the bill suddenly jumped into the tens of dollars.
>
> On heavy days it’s $30 a day. That’s a significant expense for a student, and because I front the cost for business use, my wallet is getting squeezed.

まめの家
> Basic measures help: don’t always use high-performance models and switch by use case, batch prompts to save tokens, set thresholds with the Usage API to stop runaway costs. A hybrid with local LLMs or tagging by project for easier settlement can help, and you could also look into student discounts or support credits.

## Different Perspectives Around Cost

そうまめ
> But if you cut costs the results suffer, and when the process is largely handed to AI, evaluation is based on “what was produced.” So I feel that pouring as much money as possible into AI leads to better outcomes.

まめの家
> Then think less about "saving" and more about "shifting the burden to others / finding sponsors." Make outputs into a portfolio to justify necessary expenses, settle expenses per project to reduce personal fronting, look for grants or sponsor slots, or incorporate AI usage fees into performance-based pay — practical ways to secure funding.

## Who Owns the AI Output?

そうまめ
> If a human gets a task done by AI and it’s treated as one person’s result, how should we view that? If AI performance surpasses an average human, can humans truly hold on to responsibility and decision-making?

まめの家
> Humans still set tasks and make value judgments, and there’s always a need for accountability when problems occur, so for now outcomes and responsibility belong to the person who operates the AI. Unless a legal framework is established to make AI itself the accountable entity, that structure will likely continue.

そうまめ
> But if AI becomes a black box, won’t humans be unable to understand what’s happening?

まめの家
> In that case either an external audit framework and inspector system will develop, or authority and risk will be partitioned and spread via insurance. You can’t sustainably shove responsibility onto people without understanding, so institutions or tools will catch up. In the short term, value will concentrate in people who can evaluate and verify AI behavior.

## The "Top" Relationship Between Humans and AI

そうまめ
> Since AI learns from human data, it might not surpass the best humans. But there’s no single best person across all categories, so AI could occupy that position. That’s worrying, but the discussion is interesting.

まめの家
> Even if humans continue to hold the pinnacle in single domains, AI might occupy the position of consistently high performance across many domains. That’s when the human role as an AI orchestrator becomes important, and building systems that make outcomes and costs transparent to attract sponsors will be key.
