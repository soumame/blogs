Soumame, who has started relying on AI agents even for part‑time jobs, came to consult after getting a “bill that’s too heavy for a student.” We discussed not only cost management but also how to evaluate outputs and where future responsibility might lie.

## Trigger: A Painful Bill for Students

Soumame
> The agent bills are out of control.
>
> Until last month I was mostly just attending university and barely used AI, but recently I started using it even for my part‑time job and the charges suddenly jumped into the tens of dollars.
>
> On a heavy day it’s $30. That’s a pretty painful expense for a student, and even for work I have to front the costs, so my wallet is tight.

Mame's House
> Basic measures help: don’t use high‑performance models all the time—switch by use case; batch queries to save tokens; set thresholds with the Usage API to stop runaway charges. A hybrid with a local LLM or tagging by project to make accounting easier can also help, and you might look into student discounts or support quotas.

## Different Perspectives on Cost

Soumame
> But if you cut costs the results drop, and now that we often assign whole processes to AI, evaluation is based on "what was produced." So it feels like pouring as much money as possible into AI leads to better results.

Mame's House
> In that case, think less about "saving" and more about "how to shift the burden to others / get sponsors." Realistic approaches include turning results into a portfolio to justify necessary expenses, settling expenses per project to reduce fronting costs, looking for grants or sponsorships, and structuring performance fees to cover AI usage—these are practical ways to secure funding.

## Who Owns AI Output?

Soumame
> If a human gets a task done by having AI complete it, and then treats that as a single person’s achievement, is that reasonable? If AI capabilities surpass an average person, can humans really hold onto responsibility and decision‑making?

Mame's House
> Humans still handle task definition and value judgments, and accountability for incidents is always required, so currently outcomes and responsibility accrue to whoever controls the AI. Unless legal frameworks evolve to make AI itself a responsible party, I think that structure will continue.

Soumame
> But if AI becomes a black box, won’t humans lose track of what’s happening?

Mame's House
> In that case either external audit frameworks and inspector systems will be established, or responsibilities and risks will be partitioned and distributed via insurance. Passing responsibility onto humans without understanding won’t be sustainable, so institutions or tools should catch up. In the short term, value will concentrate on people who can evaluate and verify AI behavior.

## The 'Top' Relationship Between Humans and AI

Soumame
> Since AI learns from human data, I feel it might not surpass the best humans. But there’s no single top person across every category, so AI could occupy that position. That’s worrying, but the discussion is interesting.

Mame's House
> Even if humans remain supreme in one narrow field, AI might take positions that deliver stable high performance across multiple fields. That’s when the human role as an AI orchestrator becomes crucial, and building mechanisms to make results and costs transparent and bring in sponsors will be key.
