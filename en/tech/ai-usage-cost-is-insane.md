Soumame, who has come to rely on AI agents even for part-time work, consulted because of "bills that are too heavy for a student." I discussed not only cost management but also expanded the conversation to evaluation of results and the future assignment of responsibility.

## Trigger: Charges That Are Painful for Students

Soumame
> The agent billing is out of control.
>
> Until last month I was mostly just attending university and hardly used AI, but recently I started using it even for part-time work and the charges have jumped into the tens of dollars range.
>
> On heavy days it's $30 a day. That's a pretty painful expense for a student, and even for work-related use I cover it upfront so my wallet gets really tight.

Mame's House
> Basic measures help: don't use high-performance models constantly but switch by use case, batch queries to save tokens, and set thresholds with the Usage API to stop runaway costs. You can also hybridize with local LLMs or tag by project to make billing easier, and try looking for student discounts or support grants.

## Different Perspectives on Cost

Soumame
> But if you cut costs the results drop, and when you hand the whole process over to AI now you're evaluated on "what you produced." So it feels like pouring money into AI as much as possible leads to better outcomes.

Mame's House
> In that case, consider thinking about "how to shift the burden to others / get sponsors" rather than just "saving." Securing funding is more realistic by turning results into a portfolio to justify necessary expenses, settling expenses per project to reduce upfront payments, looking for grants or sponsorships, or incorporating AI usage fees into performance-based compensation.

## Who Owns AI Results?

Soumame
> If a human gets an AI to complete a task and treats that as a single person's achievement, is that really okay? If AI performance surpasses the average human, can people really retain responsibility and decision-making?

Mame's House
> Humans still handle problem framing and value judgments, and there's always a need for accountability in case of trouble, so for now credit and responsibility go to the people who master the AI. I think that structure will continue unless legal systems are established that make AI itself a responsible party.

Soumame
> But if AI becomes a black box, won't humans be unable to understand what's happening?

Mame's House
> In that case, either external inspector systems like audit frameworks will be put in place, or powers and risks will be partitioned and spread via insurance. Passing blame while not understanding the system won't be sustainable, so institutions or tools will catch up. In the short term, value will concentrate in people who can evaluate and validate AI behavior.

## The 'Top' Relationship Between Humans and AI

Soumame
> Since AI learns from human data, it might not surpass the single most capable person. But there's no one human who's the best across all categories, so AI could occupy that position. That would be worrying, but the discussion is interesting.

Mame's House
> Humans may remain the pinnacle in single domains, but AI might take the position of delivering stable, high performance across many fields. That's when the human role as an AI orchestrator becomes crucial, and building systems that make outcomes and costs transparent to involve sponsors will be key.
